Learning to drone

How to travel with your drone

Drones have completely reshaped travel photography and videography, granting relatively cheap and easy access to angles and sights that would have been simply impossible in the past. And while they can unveil magnificent views of the world, they must be used with caution.

Use them with caution

Different destinations have different rules around the use of drones, while there are some standard operating practices that are good to get familiar with. Here's our guide to get you started.

Given that most commercial drones are now relatively small in size, it’s likely you can pack it in your carry-on or your checked luggage when flying. Larger specialist drones or higher-end ones may be outside the dimensions for carry-on, especially when in their protective cases, so it’s always good to check with your airline before flying.

Keeping expensive equipment in a carry-on case is good practice, as you have more control of how it's handled. Likewise, always pack batteries in a carry-on bag, so they can be stored in a pressurized environment where the temperature is less likely to fluctuate. Most commercial drone batteries have less than 100 Wh (watt-hours), but it’s good to check against the airline’s rules just in case.

Most commercial drone batteries have less than 100 Wh (watt-hours)

Look at the airport security practices for any country you're transiting through. When travelling with your drone through customs be aware that laws vary from country to country, and some places outright ban drones (more on that below). If you’re travelling through security to a country with strict drone laws, it goes without saying you should leave it at home for that particular trip. You could be risking fines or confiscation.
